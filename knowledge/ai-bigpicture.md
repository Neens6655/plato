Extracted from: The Coming Wave, The Human Mind Owner's Manual, McKinsey Future of Work, McKinsey GenAI Economics, The Next Big Arenas of Competition

## The Coming Wave by Mustafa Suleyman & Michael Bhaskar

### The Containment Problem
**Source:** The Coming Wave by Mustafa Suleyman & Michael Bhaskar
**Advisors:** contrarian, strategist
**One-liner:** Technology's inherent predisposition to diffuse widely makes it effectively impossible to predict, control, or contain its emergent impacts.
**Core concept:** Every foundational technology follows a law: it gets cheaper, easier to use, and proliferates. Once a general-purpose technology (like AI or synthetic biology) is created, no institution can reliably prevent it from spreading and producing unforeseen consequences.
**Audit application:** A Contrarian advisor challenges any project that assumes a sustainable moat built on proprietary technology access alone: "If your tech advantage will inevitably be commoditized, what is your durable value?"
**Red flags it detects:**
- Projects betting entire strategy on exclusive access to a technology that will inevitably proliferate
- Business plans assuming regulatory barriers will permanently prevent competitors
- Founders dismissing open-source or low-cost alternatives as non-threats

### The Dilemma (Catastrophe vs. Dystopia Fork)
**Source:** The Coming Wave by Mustafa Suleyman & Michael Bhaskar
**Advisors:** contrarian, strategist
**One-liner:** Pursuing powerful technologies leads toward catastrophic misuse; refusing them leads toward stagnation -- both paths carry existential risk.
**Core concept:** Both pursuing and not pursuing technology is fraught with risk. Technologically stagnant societies are historically unstable. The chances of navigating a "narrow path" between catastrophe and dystopia shrink as technology becomes cheaper and more pervasive.
**Audit application:** An advisor stress-tests whether a project honestly accounts for BOTH the risks of building AND the risks of someone else building it first without safety.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Projects presenting only upside without addressing catastrophic misuse scenarios
- Organizations using "responsible AI" language as marketing rather than structural commitment
- Strategies assuming they can "opt out" of a technology wave

### Four Features of the Coming Wave (Asymmetry, Hyper-evolution, Omni-use, Autonomy)
**Source:** The Coming Wave by Mustafa Suleyman & Michael Bhaskar
**Advisors:** contrarian, architect
**One-liner:** Four characteristics -- asymmetry, hyper-evolution, omni-use, and autonomy -- make AI/biotech categorically harder to contain than previous waves.
**Core concept:** Asymmetry: small groups wield enormous power. Hyper-evolution: exponential improvement outrunning institutions. Omni-use: same capability serves benign and malicious ends. Autonomy: systems operate without human supervision.
**Audit application:** Score any project touching AI or biotech against all four features. High scores on multiple features demand proportionally stronger safety architecture.
**Red flags it detects:**
- AI systems deployed autonomously with no kill switch or human-in-the-loop
- Products with obvious dual-use potential lacking usage monitoring
- Governance cadence (quarterly) mismatched with technology evolution speed (weekly)

### Fragility Amplifiers
**Source:** The Coming Wave by Mustafa Suleyman & Michael Bhaskar
**Advisors:** contrarian, strategist
**One-liner:** Coming-wave technologies amplify existing societal fragilities rather than creating new ones from scratch.
**Core concept:** AI-empowered cyberattacks, deepfakes, and automated disinformation don't create polarization out of nothing; they pour accelerant on existing fires. Organizations mistake these for novel threats when they are systemic amplifications.
**Audit application:** Evaluate whether a product could inadvertently amplify societal fragilities at scale.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Platforms amplifying engagement without measuring downstream societal effects
- AI tools deployed in contexts of existing inequality without bias audits
- Products eroding institutional trust by automating processes depending on human accountability

---

### Pessimism Aversion
**Source:** The Coming Wave by Mustafa Suleyman & Michael Bhaskar
**Advisors:** contrarian
**One-liner:** Elites systematically ignore negative narratives about technology, creating a structural blind spot in strategic planning.
**Core concept:** People with power tend to dismiss warnings as alarmist. This is embedded in VC pitch culture, corporate strategy, and policy-making, all of which reward optimistic narratives and punish cautionary ones.
**Audit application:** Audit the PROCESS of evaluation itself. Are dissenting voices being heard? Is there structural incentive suppressing honest risk assessment?
**Red flags it detects:**
- Leadership where no one has articulated a plausible failure scenario in 12 months
- Pitch decks with zero discussion of existential risks
- Cultures labeling cautious team members as "not team players"

## The Human Mind Owner's Manual by Joeel A. Rivera & Natalie Rivera

### The ABC Model of CBT
**Source:** The Human Mind Owner's Manual by Joeel A. Rivera & Natalie Rivera
**Advisors:** contrarian, strategist
**One-liner:** It is not the situation that determines our response -- it is our Belief about the situation.
**Core concept:** An Activating Event (A) triggers a Belief (B), which produces a Consequence (C). Most people mistakenly believe A directly causes C. By changing the belief, you change the outcome without changing the external situation.
**Audit application:** Diagnose whether a team is reacting to market signals through distorted beliefs. "What belief about the rejection is driving your next decision?"
**Red flags it detects:**
- Teams pivoting strategy based on emotional reactions to single data points
- Founders catastrophizing a failed launch into belief their entire thesis is wrong
- Decision-making driven by loudest emotional reaction in the room

---

### Cognitive Distortions (Systematic Thinking Errors)
**Source:** The Human Mind Owner's Manual by Joeel A. Rivera & Natalie Rivera
**Advisors:** contrarian
**One-liner:** The human mind systematically distorts reality in predictable ways -- mind-reading, catastrophizing, all-or-nothing thinking, jumping to conclusions.
**Core concept:** These are universal human defaults that operate in boardrooms just as much as personal life. Every major decision should be screened for cognitive distortions.
**Audit application:** Screen strategic decisions: Is the team catastrophizing about a competitor? Mind-reading what the market "wants" without data? All-or-nothing thinking?
**Red flags it detects:**
- Strategy documents presenting binary outcomes with no probability ranges
- Market assumptions based on what the team "feels" rather than validated research
- Competitive analysis attributing specific intentions to competitors without evidence

---

### Three Levels of Thinking (Inferences, Evaluations, Core Beliefs)
**Source:** The Human Mind Owner's Manual by Joeel A. Rivera & Natalie Rivera
**Advisors:** contrarian, architect
**One-liner:** Thought operates at three stacked levels, and effective intervention must address the correct level.
**Core concept:** Inferences are surface interpretations. Evaluations assign meaning. Core beliefs are deep rules generating both. Most interventions operate only at inference level without reaching the root.
**Audit application:** Diagnose recurring organizational dysfunctions by drilling past surface inference through evaluation to core belief.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Organizations that "learned lessons" multiple times but keep repeating mistakes
- Stated strategy contradicting revealed preferences
- Teams addressing symptoms while ignoring systemic cultural beliefs

---

### Locus of Control (Internal vs. External)
**Source:** The Human Mind Owner's Manual by Joeel A. Rivera & Natalie Rivera
**Advisors:** contrarian, strategist
**One-liner:** Internal locus of control (believing you shape outcomes) determines resilience, agency, and strategic effectiveness.
**Core concept:** Research shows individuals and organizations with internal locus of control are more resilient, proactive, and innovative. Locus of control is modifiable through deliberate action.
**Audit application:** When a team says "the market moved against us," diagnose whether they are operating from external locus that blinds them to their own agency.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Strategy narratives dominated by external explanations for underperformance
- Leadership spending more time monitoring competitors than improving own product
- Organizations responding to setbacks with blame rather than capability building

## A Future That Works (McKinsey Global Institute, 2017)

### Activity-Level Automation Analysis
**Source:** A Future That Works by McKinsey Global Institute
**Advisors:** architect, strategist
**One-liner:** Automation potential should be assessed at individual work ACTIVITIES, not entire occupations.
**Core concept:** Less than 5% of occupations can be fully automated, but 60% have at least 30% automatable activities. Most automatable: predictable physical work (81%), data processing (69%), data collection (64%). Least: managing people (9%), applying expertise (18%).
**Audit application:** Decompose target roles into constituent activities and score each for automation potential. Prevents over-promising ("AI replaces all jobs") and under-estimating.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Business cases promising to "eliminate" entire job categories
- AI plans ignoring the 40-70% of activities that cannot be automated
- ROI projections not accounting for redesign, retraining, and change management

---

### Five Factors Governing Automation Adoption Pace
**Source:** A Future That Works by McKinsey Global Institute
**Advisors:** strategist, architect
**One-liner:** Five factors -- technical feasibility, cost, labor dynamics, economic benefits, and regulatory acceptance -- collectively determine when automation actually happens.
**Core concept:** Technical feasibility is necessary but nowhere near sufficient. These five gates explain why automation's macro effects play out over decades, not years.
**Audit application:** Five-gate checklist for any automation investment. A project clearing gate 1 (technical feasibility) but failing gate 3 (cheap labor available) won't deliver projected ROI.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Automation business cases assuming adoption follows immediately from technical demonstration
- Projects ignoring labor market dynamics in their region
- Strategies assuming regulatory approval in domains where social acceptance is years away

---

### The 18 Capabilities Framework
**Source:** A Future That Works by McKinsey Global Institute
**Advisors:** architect
**One-liner:** Every work activity can be decomposed into 18 performance capabilities spanning sensory, cognitive, language, social, and physical skills.
**Core concept:** The 18 capabilities span five groups: sensory perception, cognitive (8 capabilities), natural language (2), social/emotional (3), physical (4). For each work activity, assess which capabilities are required and at what performance level.
**Audit application:** Create rigorous capability maps for any role being evaluated for AI augmentation. Specify which capabilities the AI must match, at what level, and where technology stands.
**Red flags it detects:**
- AI projects claiming to automate a role but only demonstrating capability in 3 of 12 requirements
- Vendor demos showcasing strengths while ignoring actual role requirements
- Implementation timelines assuming capability gaps close automatically

---

### Human-Machine Complementarity Thesis
**Source:** A Future That Works by McKinsey Global Institute
**Advisors:** strategist, contrarian
**One-liner:** Productivity gains from automation require humans to continue working alongside machines, not be replaced by them.
**Core concept:** The world faces a DEFICIT of human labor (demographic aging), not a surplus. Automation is needed to maintain growth -- but only if the workforce is simultaneously reskilled. The economic value of automation only materializes if people continue to work alongside machines.
**Audit application:** Challenge both doomers and zealots. Any automation strategy without a workforce transformation strategy is economically incomplete.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Automation strategies modeling cost savings from headcount reduction without redeployment plans
- Organizations investing in AI tools but not in reskilling
- Forecasts treating automation as substitute rather than complement

## The Economic Potential of Generative AI (McKinsey, 2023)

### The Four-Function Value Concentration Model
**Source:** The Economic Potential of Generative AI by McKinsey
**Advisors:** strategist, architect
**One-liner:** 75% of generative AI's total value is concentrated in four functions: customer operations, marketing/sales, software engineering, and R&D.
**Core concept:** Customer operations (30-45% productivity improvement), marketing/sales (5-15%), software engineering (20-45%), and R&D (10-15%). Total: $2.6T to $4.4T annually.
**Audit application:** Prioritize gen AI deployment in the four high-value functions. If a project's primary use case is outside these four, it needs especially rigorous justification.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Pursuing gen AI in marginal functions while ignoring the four high-value ones
- Equal investment across all functions rather than concentrating on highest ROI
- Claiming transformative gen AI impact in manufacturing (where traditional ML is the value driver)

---

### The Knowledge Work Automation Shift
**Source:** The Economic Potential of Generative AI by McKinsey
**Advisors:** contrarian, strategist
**One-liner:** Generative AI inverts the historical pattern: it impacts knowledge work MORE than physical labor.
**Core concept:** Previous waves affected lower-wage work. Gen AI's breakthrough is natural language, which accounts for 25% of total work time and concentrates in high-wage occupations. Midpoint for automating half of work shifted from 2055 to 2045.
**Audit application:** Challenge organizations assuming AI only affects "low-skill" jobs. The data shows the opposite.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Investing in AI for back-office while assuming high-paid knowledge workers are safe
- Workforce planning based on historical pattern (automation = blue collar risk)
- Underestimating how quickly gen AI-augmented competitors can outproduce them

---

### The Dual-Lens Valuation Method
**Source:** The Economic Potential of Generative AI by McKinsey
**Advisors:** architect, strategist
**One-liner:** Gen AI's value must be assessed through both specific use cases (bottom-up) AND work-activity automation potential (top-down).
**Core concept:** Lens 1: 63 specific use cases = $2.6T-$4.4T. Lens 2: broad productivity across 2,100+ activities = additional value. Combined: $6.1T-$7.9T. Organizations need BOTH targeted high-value use cases AND broad enablement.
**Audit application:** Design gen AI strategy with both lenses. One lens alone captures at most half the value.
**Red flags it detects:**
- AI strategy with only targeted use cases but no broad enablement
- Deploying ChatGPT-like tools to everyone without prioritizing specific high-value use cases
- ROI models counting only one lens

---

### Generative AI Adoption Timeline Acceleration
**Source:** The Economic Potential of Generative AI by McKinsey
**Advisors:** contrarian, strategist
**One-liner:** The midpoint for automating half of work activities shifted from 2055 to 2045 -- a full decade forward.
**Core concept:** The acceleration is driven by natural language capabilities. Range remains wide (2030-2060). Organizations that plan on a 20-year transition may have only 5-10 years.
**Audit application:** Stress-test organizational timelines. "We have time" is the most dangerous assumption.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Multi-year transformation roadmaps built on pre-2023 timeline assumptions
- Organizations "monitoring" gen AI rather than actively piloting
- Workforce development plans assuming 10-15 year transition when 5-10 may be realistic

## The Next Big Arenas of Competition (McKinsey Global Institute, 2024)

### The Arena-Creation Potion (Three Ingredients)
**Source:** The Next Big Arenas of Competition by McKinsey Global Institute
**Advisors:** strategist, architect
**One-liner:** Three ingredients -- tech/business model step changes, escalatory investment incentives, and large addressable market -- create arena industries.
**Core concept:** Arenas are defined by outsize growth AND outsize market share dynamism (shuffle rate). When all three ingredients combine, competition becomes a tournament with escalating stakes and increasing returns to scale.
**Audit application:** Score any target market against the three ingredients. All three present = invest aggressively. Missing one = different strategy.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Massive escalatory investments in markets lacking arena ingredients
- Assuming industry stability when all three ingredients are present
- Treating high-growth market as arena when it lacks escalatory investment incentives

---

### The Six Arena Differentiators
**Source:** The Next Big Arenas of Competition by McKinsey Global Institute
**Advisors:** strategist, contrarian
**One-liner:** Arenas capture outsize profit, attract disproportionate R&D, enable new entrants, spawn global giants, tend toward concentration, and operate globally.
**Core concept:** Arenas went from 9% to 49% of global economic profit. 34% of arena market cap was held by 2005 outsiders. Eight of top ten companies by market cap in 2020 were in arenas.
**Audit application:** Score company positioning against six metrics. New entrants should focus on arenas during early formation.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Applying non-arena playbooks in markets that have transitioned to arena dynamics
- Entering arenas during concentration phase rather than early formation
- Regional companies in global arenas without global strategy

---

### The 18 Arenas of Tomorrow (2040 Landscape Map)
**Source:** The Next Big Arenas of Competition by McKinsey Global Institute
**Advisors:** strategist, architect
**One-liner:** Eighteen industries could generate $29T-$48T in revenues by 2040, growing from 4% to 10-16% of global GDP.
**Core concept:** Categories: continuing arenas (e-commerce, EVs, cloud), spin-off arenas (AI software, digital ads, streaming), and emergent arenas (autonomous vehicles, space, cybersecurity, batteries, robotics). Three swing factors: geopolitics, AI adoption pace, green transition speed.
**Audit application:** Map any venture against these 18 arenas. Are you in a forming arena? Adjacent to one? In one losing status?
**Red flags it detects:**
- Investments concentrated in industries losing arena status
- Ventures not recognizing adjacency to a forming arena
- Portfolio strategies lacking exposure to any of the 18 arenas

---

### The Shuffle Rate
**Source:** The Next Big Arenas of Competition by McKinsey Global Institute
**Advisors:** contrarian, strategist
**One-liner:** The degree to which market share positions change hands is the best leading indicator of arena dynamics.
**Core concept:** High shuffle rate + high growth = arena. Example: consumer electronics 2005-2020, Apple went from 5% to 38% while LG went from 22% to 8%.
**Audit application:** When an incumbent claims their position is secure: "What is the shuffle rate in your industry? Are you Apple or are you LG?"
**Red flags it detects:**
- Market leaders citing current share as evidence of durability without acknowledging shuffle rate
- Investment theses assuming stable positions in high-shuffle-rate industries
- Strategic plans without scenarios for dramatic market share shifts
