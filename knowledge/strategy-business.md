# Plato Brain: Strategy & Business Frameworks

Extracted from: Zero to One, The Lean Startup, The $100 Startup, From Impossible to Inevitable, The Startup Owner's Manual, Rework

---

## Zero to One by Peter Thiel

### The Zero-to-One vs. One-to-N Test
**Source:** Zero to One by Peter Thiel
**Advisors:** strategist, contrarian
**One-liner:** Determine whether you are creating something genuinely new (0 to 1) or merely copying what exists (1 to n).
**Core concept:** Horizontal progress (1 to n) means copying things that work -- globalization, cloning existing models. Vertical progress (0 to 1) means doing something nobody has done before -- true technology invention. Companies that only do horizontal progress compete on price and eventually see profits erode. Only vertical progress creates durable value.
**Audit application:** The strategist asks: "Is this project creating a new category or copying an existing one with minor tweaks?" If the answer is incremental improvement in a crowded space, the project lacks defensibility and long-term upside. The contrarian asks: "What important truth do very few people agree with you on?" If the founder cannot articulate a genuine contrarian insight, they are likely building a 1-to-n business.
**Red flags it detects:**
- The business is described as "Uber for X" or "Airbnb for Y" with no structural innovation
- The founder cannot name a belief they hold that most people disagree with
- The product competes primarily on price or incremental features in an established market

### The Monopoly vs. Competition Framework
**Source:** Zero to One by Peter Thiel
**Advisors:** strategist, contrarian
**One-liner:** All happy companies are different (monopolies); all unhappy companies are the same (competing in commodity markets).
**Core concept:** Capitalism and competition are opposites. Under perfect competition, all profits get competed away. The goal is to build a monopoly -- a company so good at what it does that no other firm can offer a close substitute. Monopolists disguise their monopoly by framing their market as a union of large markets. Non-monopolists exaggerate their distinction by defining their market as a narrow intersection.
**Audit application:** The strategist forces the founder to define their market honestly: "If you removed the word 'for' from your market description, how big is the competitive field?" Check whether the founder is claiming uniqueness through an intersection fallacy rather than actual market dominance.
**Red flags it detects:**
- The founder defines their market as the intersection of several tiny niches to claim they "own" it
- There are many direct competitors and the founder dismisses them as "not really the same"
- The business creates value for customers but captures almost none of it (airline economics)

### The Seven Questions Every Business Must Answer
**Source:** Zero to One by Peter Thiel
**Advisors:** strategist, contrarian, customer
**One-liner:** A startup must pass seven critical tests covering technology, timing, monopoly, people, distribution, durability, and secrets.
**Core concept:** (1) The Engineering Question -- Can you create breakthrough technology, not just incremental improvement? (2) The Timing Question -- Is now the right time to start? (3) The Monopoly Question -- Are you starting with a big share of a small market? (4) The People Question -- Do you have the right team? (5) The Distribution Question -- Do you have a way to deliver your product? (6) The Durability Question -- Will your market position be defensible in 10-20 years? (7) The Secret Question -- Have you identified a unique opportunity others don't see?
**Audit application:** The strategist runs every project through all seven questions. A "no" on any single question is a serious risk. A "no" on several is fatal.
**Red flags it detects:**
- The technology is only 10% better than alternatives (must be 10x)
- There is no clear distribution strategy ("if you build it they will come" thinking)
- The founder cannot articulate a secret -- a non-obvious insight about the market

### The Anti-Dogma Framework
**Source:** Zero to One by Peter Thiel
**Advisors:** contrarian
**One-liner:** The conventional lessons learned from the dot-com crash are exactly wrong, and following them leads to mediocrity.
**Core concept:** After the 2000 crash, Silicon Valley adopted four dogmas: (1) Make incremental advances, (2) Stay lean and flexible, (3) Improve on the competition, (4) Focus on product, not sales. Thiel argues the opposite principles are more correct: (1) It is better to risk boldness than triviality, (2) A bad plan is better than no plan, (3) Competitive markets destroy profits, (4) Sales matters just as much as product.
**Audit application:** The contrarian advisor checks whether the founder is being bold or timid because of inherited startup dogma. Are they "iterating" endlessly without conviction? Are they avoiding sales?
**Red flags it detects:**
- The founder treats "lean" as a synonym for "no plan" and "no conviction"
- The startup has no sales or distribution strategy, relying entirely on viral or organic growth
- The founder is afraid to make bold claims about the future

### Last Mover Advantage
**Source:** Zero to One by Peter Thiel
**Advisors:** strategist
**One-liner:** Being the last to innovate in a market -- making the defining move -- is far more valuable than being the first mover.
**Core concept:** First-mover advantage is overrated. What matters is generating cash flows in the future. You want to be the last mover -- the company that makes the last great development and enjoys years of monopoly profits. This requires proprietary technology, network effects, economies of scale, and strong branding.
**Audit application:** The strategist asks: "Will this company still dominate this market in 10 years? What structural moats protect it?"
**Red flags it detects:**
- The entire strategy depends on "getting there first" with no structural moat
- The business has no network effects, switching costs, or proprietary technology
- Cash flow projections show most value in the near term rather than compounding over decades

## The Lean Startup by Eric Ries

### The Build-Measure-Learn Feedback Loop
**Source:** The Lean Startup by Eric Ries
**Advisors:** strategist, customer
**One-liner:** The fundamental engine of a startup is a continuous cycle of building a product, measuring customer response, and learning whether to pivot or persevere.
**Core concept:** Every startup's core activity is to turn ideas into products, measure how customers respond, and learn whether to pivot or persevere. The goal is to minimize total time through this loop. Speed through this loop is the primary competitive advantage of a startup.
**Audit application:** The customer advisor asks: "How fast is your feedback loop? How many days between a new hypothesis and data from real customers?"
**Red flags it detects:**
- The team has been building for months with no customer contact or behavioral data
- Metrics are vanity metrics (total signups, page views) rather than actionable metrics (activation rate, retention)
- The team cannot articulate what they learned from the last cycle

---

### The Minimum Viable Product (MVP)
**Source:** The Lean Startup by Eric Ries
**Advisors:** customer, strategist
**One-liner:** Build the smallest possible version of your product that lets you start the Build-Measure-Learn loop with real customers.
**Core concept:** The MVP is not a minimal product -- it is the fastest way to get through the Build-Measure-Learn feedback loop with the minimum amount of effort. It tests your riskiest assumptions first, before you invest heavily.
**Audit application:** The customer advisor asks: "What is the riskiest assumption in your business model, and how does this MVP test it?"
**Red flags it detects:**
- The team has been building for 6+ months without shipping anything to real users
- The MVP tests the easy assumptions rather than the riskiest leap-of-faith assumptions
- The founder says "we need to add one more feature before we can launch"

---

### Validated Learning
**Source:** The Lean Startup by Eric Ries
**Advisors:** strategist, customer
**One-liner:** The only meaningful measure of startup progress is validated learning -- empirical proof that you are reducing uncertainty about your business model.
**Core concept:** Startups exist to learn how to build a sustainable business. Traditional measures of progress (shipping features, hitting milestones) are meaningless if you are building something nobody wants.
**Audit application:** The strategist asks: "What have you validated in the last 30 days? Show me the data."
**Red flags it detects:**
- Progress is measured in features shipped rather than customer behavior
- The team uses "we learned a lot" as a euphemism for "nothing worked and we have no data"
- There is no systematic experimentation

---

### The Pivot-or-Persevere Decision
**Source:** The Lean Startup by Eric Ries
**Advisors:** strategist, contrarian
**One-liner:** At regular intervals, a startup must honestly decide whether to pivot or persevere on the current course.
**Core concept:** A pivot is a structured course correction designed to test a new fundamental hypothesis. It requires honest metrics, not vanity metrics.
**Audit application:** The contrarian advisor asks: "Are you persevering because the data supports it, or because you are emotionally attached to your original vision?"
**Red flags it detects:**
- The team has been on the same strategy for 12+ months with flat or declining metrics
- There are no pre-defined pivot criteria or regular pivot-or-persevere meetings
- The founder conflates pivoting with failure

---

### Innovation Accounting
**Source:** The Lean Startup by Eric Ries
**Advisors:** strategist
**One-liner:** Replace traditional financial accounting with a startup-specific system that measures progress toward a sustainable business model.
**Core concept:** Innovation accounting works in three steps: (1) Use an MVP to establish baseline real data. (2) Tune the engine -- run experiments to improve metrics. (3) Pivot or persevere based on whether experiments move the needle.
**Audit application:** The strategist asks: "What is your baseline metric, what is your target, and what experiments are you running to close the gap?"
**Red flags it detects:**
- The startup reports "growth" using cumulative totals instead of per-cohort metrics
- There is no baseline measurement
- Experiments are run but never connected to specific metric improvements

## The $100 Startup by Chris Guillebeau

### The Convergence Formula (Passion + Skill + Usefulness)
**Source:** The $100 Startup by Chris Guillebeau
**Advisors:** customer
**One-liner:** A viable microbusiness exists at the intersection of what you love, what you are good at, and what other people will pay for.
**Core concept:** Passion alone is not enough. The key question is not "What do I love?" but "What do I love that also solves a problem for others?"
**Audit application:** The customer advisor asks: "Who exactly will pay for this, and what specific problem does it solve for them?"
**Red flags it detects:**
- The founder is passionate but cannot name a single paying customer or target persona
- The business idea solves a problem the founder has but nobody else does
- There is no evidence of willingness to pay

---

### The One-Page Business Plan
**Source:** The $100 Startup by Chris Guillebeau
**Advisors:** strategist
**One-liner:** If your business plan is longer than one page, it is too long.
**Core concept:** The one-page business plan forces clarity: What do you sell? Who buys it? Why do they buy it? How will they pay? If you cannot explain the business in one page, you do not understand it well enough.
**Audit application:** The strategist asks the founder to write down the business on a single page. If they struggle, the idea is not ready.
**Red flags it detects:**
- The founder needs a 20-slide deck to explain what the business does
- Core questions cannot be answered in one sentence each
- The business model depends on complex multi-sided dynamics the founder cannot simplify

---

### The Value-Based Pricing Framework
**Source:** The $100 Startup by Chris Guillebeau
**Advisors:** customer, strategist
**One-liner:** Price based on the value your product creates for customers, not on how much it costs you to produce.
**Core concept:** The correct approach is to understand the transformation you provide to the customer and price based on that value.
**Audit application:** The customer advisor asks: "What is the dollar value of the outcome your customer gets?" Then compares it to the price.
**Red flags it detects:**
- The product is priced based on competitor pricing or cost-plus rather than value delivered
- The founder is afraid to charge more
- There is no clear articulation of the transformation the customer receives

## From Impossible to Inevitable by Aaron Ross & Jason Lemkin

### The Nail-a-Niche Framework
**Source:** From Impossible to Inevitable by Aaron Ross & Jason Lemkin
**Advisors:** strategist, customer
**One-liner:** Hyper-growth begins by dominating a single, tightly defined niche before expanding outward.
**Core concept:** Most companies fail because they try to serve a broad market before dominating a narrow one. You analyze your best existing customers for patterns, build a buyer persona, and create messaging that speaks directly to that segment.
**Audit application:** The customer advisor asks: "Can you describe your ideal customer in one sentence with at least three specific attributes?"
**Red flags it detects:**
- The target market is described as "SMBs" or "enterprises" with no further specificity
- The product has features for five different customer types but excels for none
- Marketing messaging is generic

---

### The Three Engines of Lead Generation (Seeds, Nets, Spears)
**Source:** From Impossible to Inevitable by Aaron Ross & Jason Lemkin
**Advisors:** strategist
**One-liner:** Predictable revenue requires three distinct engines -- word-of-mouth (seeds), inbound marketing (nets), and outbound prospecting (spears).
**Core concept:** Seeds are organic referrals. Nets are inbound marketing. Spears are targeted outbound. A company needs all three but must understand which one is primary.
**Audit application:** The strategist asks: "Which of the three engines is your primary growth driver today? What percentage of revenue comes from each?"
**Red flags it detects:**
- 100% of leads come from one source (single point of failure)
- There is no outbound sales motion
- Customer referrals are near zero

---

### The Predictable Revenue Framework
**Source:** From Impossible to Inevitable by Aaron Ross & Jason Lemkin
**Advisors:** strategist
**One-liner:** Revenue becomes predictable only when you build a repeatable system with consistent inputs producing consistent outputs.
**Core concept:** Predictable revenue means building a sales machine where X prospecting activities produce Y qualified opportunities, which produce Z closed deals.
**Audit application:** The strategist asks: "If your best salesperson quit tomorrow, what would happen to revenue?"
**Red flags it detects:**
- Revenue depends on 1-2 rockstar salespeople or the founder's personal network
- There are no documented sales processes
- Pipeline metrics are unknown or not tracked

## The Startup Owner's Manual by Steve Blank & Bob Dorf

### The Customer Development Model
**Source:** The Startup Owner's Manual by Steve Blank & Bob Dorf
**Advisors:** customer, strategist
**One-liner:** A startup is a temporary organization searching for a scalable, repeatable, profitable business model through four iterative steps.
**Core concept:** (1) Customer Discovery -- test problem/solution fit. (2) Customer Validation -- test repeatable sales. (3) Customer Creation -- drive demand. (4) Company Building -- transition to execution. Steps 1-2 are search. Steps 3-4 are execution. Most startups fail because they jump to execution before completing the search.
**Audit application:** The customer advisor asks: "Which step are you in -- are you still searching or already executing?"
**Red flags it detects:**
- The startup is scaling sales and marketing before confirming product-market fit
- The founders have not talked to at least 50-100 potential customers
- The team is executing a business plan rather than searching for a business model

---

### The Business Model Canvas as Hypothesis Map
**Source:** The Startup Owner's Manual by Steve Blank & Bob Dorf
**Advisors:** strategist
**One-liner:** Replace the static business plan with a dynamic Business Model Canvas where every box represents an untested hypothesis.
**Core concept:** Nine boxes, each representing a hypothesis. The founder's job is to systematically test each hypothesis by getting out of the building.
**Audit application:** The strategist asks: "Show me your canvas. Which boxes have been validated with real customer data?"
**Red flags it detects:**
- The team has a 40-page business plan but no Business Model Canvas
- "Validation" means the founding team agreed with each other, not that customers confirmed it
- The canvas has not changed in months

---

### The "Get Out of the Building" Imperative
**Source:** The Startup Owner's Manual by Steve Blank & Bob Dorf
**Advisors:** customer
**One-liner:** No business model survives first contact with customers -- founders must physically leave the building and talk to real people.
**Core concept:** Facts live outside the building, not inside it. Founders must personally talk to potential customers to learn, not to sell.
**Audit application:** The customer advisor asks: "How many customer conversations have the founders personally had in the last 30 days?"
**Red flags it detects:**
- The founders have not left the building in weeks or months
- All customer "research" has been done through surveys rather than face-to-face conversations
- The founders delegated customer discovery to junior employees

## Rework by Jason Fried & David Heinemeier Hansson

### Build Half a Product, Not a Half-Assed Product
**Source:** Rework by Jason Fried & David Heinemeier Hansson
**Advisors:** strategist, customer
**One-liner:** It is better to do fewer things exceptionally well than to do many things poorly.
**Core concept:** Ruthlessly cut scope until you have a product that does one or two things extraordinarily well. Start at the "epicenter" -- the one thing your product absolutely must do.
**Audit application:** The customer advisor asks: "What is the one thing this product must do brilliantly?"
**Red flags it detects:**
- The product roadmap has 20+ features for V1
- The founder cannot name the single most important thing
- The team is adding features to match competitors

---

### Underdo Your Competition
**Source:** Rework by Jason Fried & David Heinemeier Hansson
**Advisors:** contrarian, strategist
**One-liner:** Instead of one-upping competitors with more features, do less than them -- solve simpler problems more elegantly.
**Core concept:** Deliberately do less. Solve the simple problems and leave the complex ones to competitors. Avoid the "cold war of feature escalation."
**Audit application:** The contrarian advisor asks: "Are you adding this feature because customers need it or because a competitor has it?"
**Red flags it detects:**
- The product roadmap is driven by competitor feature comparisons
- The team cannot explain why their product is simpler or more focused
- Every customer request gets added to the backlog without filtering

---

### Out-Teach Your Competition
**Source:** Rework by Jason Fried & David Heinemeier Hansson
**Advisors:** strategist, customer
**One-liner:** Instead of spending on advertising, teach your customers something valuable.
**Core concept:** Teaching creates a bond with potential customers that advertising never can. By teaching, you build an audience before you have a product to sell.
**Audit application:** The customer advisor asks: "What does your company teach? What do people learn from engaging with you before they buy?"
**Red flags it detects:**
- The company has no blog, no educational content, and no public presence
- All marketing budget goes to paid acquisition with no investment in owned audience
- The founders have zero public followers or community

---

### Scratch Your Own Itch
**Source:** Rework by Jason Fried & David Heinemeier Hansson
**Advisors:** customer, contrarian
**One-liner:** The easiest way to build a great product is to solve a problem you personally have.
**Core concept:** When you build something to solve your own problem, you are your own customer. You know the pain intimately and can evaluate quality directly.
**Audit application:** The customer advisor asks: "Do you use this product every day? Would you pay for it if someone else built it?"
**Red flags it detects:**
- The founder has never personally experienced the problem
- The product is built for a customer demographic the founding team does not understand
- The team relies on surveys rather than lived experience
