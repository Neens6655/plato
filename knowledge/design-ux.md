# Plato Brain: Design & UX Frameworks

Extracted from: Hooked, Sketching User Experiences, UX for Lean Startups, Universal Principles of Design, Designing Brand Identity, Guide to Great Logos, Digital Colour in Graphic Design

## Hooked by Nir Eyal

### The Hook Model (Trigger > Action > Variable Reward > Investment)
**Source:** Hooked by Nir Eyal
**Advisors:** designer, customer
**One-liner:** A four-phase loop that turns product usage into an automatic habit through repeated cycles.
**Core concept:** Products form habits by cycling users through four stages: a Trigger (external or internal cue), an Action (simplest behavior in anticipation of reward), a Variable Reward (unpredictable payoff that creates craving), and an Investment (user puts something in that improves the next cycle). The more cycles completed, the stronger the habit.
**Audit application:** An advisor maps any product against the four phases. If a phase is missing or weak, the product will fail to form habits and will depend on expensive re-engagement marketing forever.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Product relies entirely on external triggers (ads, emails) with no path to internal triggers
- Reward is predictable and static, generating no dopamine-driven curiosity
- No investment mechanism that loads the next trigger or stores user value

---

### Internal vs. External Triggers
**Source:** Hooked by Nir Eyal
**Advisors:** designer, customer
**One-liner:** Habit-forming products graduate from external prompts to emotions and routines that automatically cue usage.
**Core concept:** External triggers are paid or earned cues (notifications, ads, links). Internal triggers are emotions, routines, or situations already in the user's life. A product succeeds long-term only when it attaches to an internal trigger so users come back unprompted.
**Audit application:** An advisor asks: "What internal trigger does this product own?" If the team cannot name the specific emotion or situation that cues usage, the product has no habit path.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Team describes growth strategy entirely in terms of ad spend and push notifications
- No research into the user's emotional state before they open the product
- Product has no answer to "why would someone come back tomorrow without being reminded?"

---

### Variable Reward and the Three Types of Variability
**Source:** Hooked by Nir Eyal
**Advisors:** designer, customer
**One-liner:** Unpredictable rewards -- of the tribe, the hunt, or the self -- create craving that fixed feedback loops cannot.
**Core concept:** There are three types: Rewards of the Tribe (social validation), Rewards of the Hunt (material or informational gains), and Rewards of the Self (mastery, completion). The best products layer multiple types.
**Audit application:** An advisor evaluates whether the product delivers variation in its reward structure and which of the three reward types are present.
**Red flags it detects:**
- User experience is identical every session with no novelty or surprise
- Social features exist but produce no visible social validation feedback
- Users have nothing to "hunt for" -- no discovery, no feed, no variable information payoff

---

### The Investment Phase
**Source:** Hooked by Nir Eyal
**Advisors:** designer, customer
**One-liner:** When users invest effort, data, or social capital into a product, they increase the likelihood of returning.
**Core concept:** After receiving a reward, users are asked to put something back in: content, data, followers, reputation, or skill. This investment loads the next trigger and stores value that makes switching costly.
**Audit application:** An advisor checks whether the product asks users to invest after reward delivery and whether that investment creates switching costs.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Product delivers value but never asks users to contribute anything back
- User data and customization are not stored or leveraged across sessions
- No network effects, no accumulated reputation -- switching cost is zero

## Sketching User Experiences by Bill Buxton

### Getting the Design Right vs. Getting the Right Design
**Source:** Sketching User Experiences by Bill Buxton
**Advisors:** designer
**One-liner:** There are two distinct design challenges: validating you are solving the right problem, and then executing the solution well.
**Core concept:** "Getting the right design" means refining a known solution. "Getting the design right" means exploring broadly to ensure you are solving the correct problem. Most teams jump to execution without validating the problem space.
**Audit application:** An advisor asks at what stage the team committed to their current direction. If no divergent exploration happened before convergence, the team may be beautifully solving the wrong problem.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Team went from brief to high-fidelity mockup with no exploratory sketching phase
- No evidence of alternative concepts being considered or killed
- Product solves a problem nobody validated with actual users

---

### Sketches Are Not Prototypes
**Source:** Sketching User Experiences by Bill Buxton
**Advisors:** designer
**One-liner:** Sketches explore the problem space cheaply; prototypes test specific solutions -- conflating the two kills innovation.
**Core concept:** Sketches are quick, disposable, ambiguous, and meant to suggest possibilities. Prototypes are specific, testable, and meant to resolve questions. Using prototypes too early locks teams into solutions before exploration is complete.
**Audit application:** An advisor evaluates whether the team used the right fidelity artifact at each stage.
**Red flags it detects:**
- No low-fidelity sketching artifacts exist in the project history
- Team built a functional prototype before exploring alternatives
- Design exploration was done entirely in high-fidelity tools making pivots expensive

---

### The Cycle of Innovation (Design Funnel)
**Source:** Sketching User Experiences by Bill Buxton
**Advisors:** designer
**One-liner:** Innovation requires deliberate alternation between divergent exploration (many ideas) and convergent refinement (one solution).
**Core concept:** The design process should alternate between generating many possibilities (divergent) and narrowing to the best option (convergent). Most organizations only do convergence.
**Audit application:** An advisor looks for evidence of both divergent and convergent phases. Were at least 3-5 concepts explored before convergence?
**Red flags it detects:**
- Only one concept was ever considered
- The "exploration" phase produced minor variations of the same idea
- Leadership demanded a solution immediately, skipping divergent phase

## UX for Lean Startups by Laura Klein

### The Lean UX Loop (Hypothesis > Build > Measure > Learn)
**Source:** UX for Lean Startups by Laura Klein
**Advisors:** designer, customer
**One-liner:** Every design change is a hypothesis to be validated with real user data, not a feature to be declared "done."
**Core concept:** Instead of building features from a vision, Lean UX treats every product change as a hypothesis: "We believe [change] will cause [user behavior] which will improve [metric]."
**Audit application:** An advisor asks for any feature: "What hypothesis did this test? What metric moved? What did you learn?"
**Red flags it detects:**
- Features are shipped and never measured for impact
- The team defines success as "feature launched" rather than "metric improved"
- No process exists to kill or iterate on features that failed

---

### MVP as Cupcake, Not Half-Baked Cake
**Source:** UX for Lean Startups by Laura Klein
**Advisors:** designer, customer
**One-liner:** A Minimum Viable Product must be both Minimum AND Viable -- a complete small thing, not an incomplete big thing.
**Core concept:** An MVP is not a half-built cake. It is a complete cupcake -- small in scope but fully realized and delightful. Cutting scope is about removing features, not removing quality.
**Audit application:** An advisor evaluates whether the MVP provides a complete, usable experience for at least one core use case.
**Red flags it detects:**
- MVP is so stripped down that no user can accomplish a real task
- Quality was sacrificed along with scope
- Team conflates "minimum" with "bad"

---

### Designing for Validation (Not for Vision)
**Source:** UX for Lean Startups by Laura Klein
**Advisors:** designer, customer
**One-liner:** Design decisions should be structured to produce measurable learning, not to fulfill a product owner's aesthetic vision.
**Core concept:** Every design artifact should be constructed to validate or invalidate a specific assumption. If you cannot articulate what you will learn from building something, do not build it.
**Audit application:** An advisor reviews the design process: does each artifact have a stated hypothesis and success metric?
**Red flags it detects:**
- Design decisions justified with "the founder wants it" rather than user evidence
- No A/B testing, usability testing, or analytics instrumentation
- The team has never killed a design based on data

## Universal Principles of Design by Lidwell, Holden, Butler

### The 80/20 Rule (Pareto Principle for Design)
**Source:** Universal Principles of Design by Lidwell, Holden, Butler
**Advisors:** designer
**One-liner:** 80% of a product's usage comes from 20% of its features -- design resources should focus there.
**Core concept:** Roughly 80% of user activity concentrates on 20% of features. Design and testing resources should focus primarily on these critical features.
**Audit application:** An advisor identifies which features account for the majority of user engagement and checks whether design investment is proportionally allocated.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Equal design attention given to all features regardless of usage data
- Rarely-used features clutter the interface and increase cognitive load
- Team cannot identify which 20% of features drive 80% of user value

---

### Aesthetic-Usability Effect
**Source:** Universal Principles of Design by Lidwell, Holden, Butler
**Advisors:** designer, customer
**One-liner:** Users perceive more attractive designs as easier to use -- and this perception persists even when proven wrong.
**Core concept:** People judge attractive products as more usable, whether or not they actually are. Aesthetic designs foster positive emotional relationships that make users more tolerant of problems.
**Audit application:** An advisor evaluates whether visual polish matches functional quality. A product that works well but looks bad will be perceived as hard to use.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Functional product with poor visual design losing users who never discover its usability
- Team dismisses visual design as "cosmetic" while users abandon due to first impressions
- Ugly interface amplifies every small friction

---

### Hick's Law (Decision Complexity)
**Source:** Universal Principles of Design by Lidwell, Holden, Butler
**Advisors:** designer, customer
**One-liner:** The time to make a decision increases logarithmically with the number of choices presented.
**Core concept:** Every additional option increases cognitive load and decision time. The remedy is to reduce options through progressive disclosure, smart defaults, and hierarchical organization.
**Audit application:** An advisor counts the number of choices on critical screens. If key screens present more than 5-7 options without hierarchy, decision fatigue is degrading the experience.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Navigation menus with 15+ items of equal visual weight
- Settings screens that expose every option at once
- Users frequently abandon flows at decision-heavy screens

---

### Progressive Disclosure
**Source:** Universal Principles of Design by Lidwell, Holden, Butler
**Advisors:** designer, customer
**One-liner:** Present only the information and controls relevant to the current task; reveal complexity only when needed.
**Core concept:** Complex systems become usable by showing only the minimum at each stage and progressively revealing more. This reduces cognitive load for novices while keeping advanced features accessible.
**Audit application:** An advisor evaluates whether the interface strategically layers information. Do new users see a simple starting point?
**Red flags it detects:**
- All features and settings visible simultaneously
- New users are overwhelmed by expert-level controls on first encounter
- No clear primary action on any screen

---

### Affordance
**Source:** Universal Principles of Design by Lidwell, Holden, Butler
**Advisors:** designer, customer
**One-liner:** The physical or visual properties of an object should suggest how it is meant to be used.
**Core concept:** When an element's appearance matches its function, users know instinctively how to interact with it. Interactive elements must visually communicate their interactivity.
**Audit application:** An advisor inspects the interface for elements that look interactive but are not, and elements that are interactive but do not look it.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Flat design where buttons, links, and static text are visually indistinguishable
- Users tap non-interactive elements because they look actionable
- Interactive elements are hidden because they lack visual affordance cues

## Designing Brand Identity by Alina Wheeler

### The Brand Identity Process (5 Phases)
**Source:** Designing Brand Identity by Alina Wheeler
**Advisors:** designer, customer
**One-liner:** Brand identity is built through five phases: research, strategy, design, touchpoints, and asset management.
**Core concept:** (1) Conducting Research (2) Clarifying Strategy (3) Designing Identity (4) Creating Touchpoints (5) Managing Assets. Skipping any phase produces a fragile brand.
**Audit application:** An advisor evaluates which phases were completed. Most failing brands skipped phases 1-2 and jumped to design.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Logo was designed without competitive audit or brand strategy
- Identity is applied inconsistently across touchpoints
- No brand guidelines exist

---

### Sequence of Cognition (Shape > Color > Content)
**Source:** Designing Brand Identity by Alina Wheeler
**Advisors:** designer, customer
**One-liner:** The brain processes visual identity in a fixed order: shape first, then color, then content.
**Core concept:** A strong brand identity must be recognizable at each cognitive level: its silhouette must be distinctive, its color palette ownable, and its name legible and memorable.
**Audit application:** An advisor tests brand identity at each cognitive level.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Logo is unrecognizable as a tiny silhouette (shape fails)
- Brand colors are identical to top competitors (color fails)
- Name is difficult to read or easily confused (content fails)

---

### Brand Touchpoint Consistency
**Source:** Designing Brand Identity by Alina Wheeler
**Advisors:** designer, customer
**One-liner:** Every customer interaction is a touchpoint, and inconsistency erodes brand trust.
**Core concept:** A brand is the sum of every interaction: website, email, packaging, signage, social media, customer service. Consistency builds recognition, trust, and loyalty.
**Audit application:** An advisor catalogs all brand touchpoints and evaluates visual and tonal consistency.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Website looks modern but printed materials use an outdated logo
- Social media has a different tone and visual style than the main website
- Different departments produce materials that look like different companies

---

### Differentiation as Brand Imperative
**Source:** Designing Brand Identity by Alina Wheeler
**Advisors:** designer, customer
**One-liner:** A brand that looks, sounds, and feels like its competitors has no reason to be chosen.
**Core concept:** The most critical function of brand identity is to differentiate. The best brand strategies "deflect the competition" by being so distinct they create their own category.
**Audit application:** An advisor performs a competitive audit: place the brand alongside its top 5 competitors. Can a stranger immediately distinguish them?
**Red flags it detects:**
- Brand identity could be swapped with a competitor's and nobody would notice
- Visual system follows category conventions without any distinctive element
- Team cannot articulate what makes their brand unique

## The Guide to Great Logos by Steve Douglas

### The Simple Logo Principle
**Source:** The Guide to Great Logos by Steve Douglas
**Advisors:** designer
**One-liner:** A great logo must work at any size, in any color mode, on any medium -- and only simple logos pass this test.
**Core concept:** Logos must reproduce well across wildly different contexts. Complex logos break down at small sizes, cost more to reproduce, and are harder to recognize. The strongest logos in history are radically simple.
**Audit application:** An advisor tests the logo at extreme sizes, in black and white, and on diverse backgrounds.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Logo becomes an unreadable blob at small sizes
- Logo requires full color to be recognizable
- Logo contains fine details that disappear in print

---

### The Fourteen Things Not to Do (Anti-Pattern Checklist)
**Source:** The Guide to Great Logos by Steve Douglas
**Advisors:** designer
**One-liner:** A checklist of common logo design failures used as a rapid quality gate.
**Core concept:** Common mistakes include: using clip art, relying on trends, too complex, too many fonts, ignoring reproduction constraints, designing by committee.
**Audit application:** An advisor runs any logo through the 14-point anti-pattern list. More than 2-3 violations means the logo needs replacement.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Logo uses trendy effects that will date it within 2 years
- Logo requires verbal explanation to understand
- Logo was designed by committee consensus

## Digital Colour in Graphic Design by Ken Pender

### Additive vs. Subtractive Color Models (RGB vs. CMYK)
**Source:** Digital Colour in Graphic Design by Ken Pender
**Advisors:** designer
**One-liner:** Screen color (RGB) and print color (CMYK) operate on fundamentally different physics, and designs must account for both.
**Core concept:** Colors that look vibrant on screen may look dull or shift hue in print. Designers must work in the correct color space for the output medium.
**Audit application:** An advisor checks whether brand colors were specified for both RGB and CMYK.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Brand guidelines only specify hex/RGB values with no CMYK
- Printed materials have visibly different colors than the website
- Vibrant screen colors chosen without checking print reproducibility

---

### Simultaneous Contrast
**Source:** Digital Colour in Graphic Design by Ken Pender
**Advisors:** designer
**One-liner:** The perceived color of any element changes dramatically based on the colors surrounding it.
**Core concept:** The same color appears lighter, darker, warmer, or cooler depending on what surrounds it. Designers must test color choices in context, not in isolation.
**Audit application:** An advisor evaluates whether color choices were tested in their actual usage contexts.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Brand color looks great in the style guide but washes out against the actual UI background
- Text legibility degrades on colored backgrounds never tested
- Adjacent UI elements become visually indistinct

---

### Color and Legibility
**Source:** Digital Colour in Graphic Design by Ken Pender
**Advisors:** designer, customer
**One-liner:** Text legibility depends critically on the contrast between text and background color.
**Core concept:** Low contrast renders text unreadable regardless of font quality. Color blindness (affecting up to 7% of males) means red-green distinctions cannot carry meaning alone.
**Audit application:** An advisor tests all text elements against WCAG contrast ratios and checks that color is never the only differentiator.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Body text has insufficient contrast (fails WCAG AA 4.5:1)
- Error/success states distinguished only by red vs. green
- Light-on-light or dark-on-dark combinations strain readability

---

### Color Temperature and Emotional Response
**Source:** Digital Colour in Graphic Design by Ken Pender
**Advisors:** designer, customer
**One-liner:** Colors have measurable subliminal effects on human emotion that designers must harness deliberately.
**Core concept:** Green is calming, red creates urgency, warm combinations feel welcoming. Designers must choose palettes that align with intended emotional response.
**Audit application:** An advisor evaluates whether the color palette's emotional tone matches the brand positioning.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Color palette emotionally contradicts the brand (e.g., aggressive colors for a meditation app)
- Colors chosen for aesthetics without considering psychological impact
- Design tested only on one monitor in one lighting condition
