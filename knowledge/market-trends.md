## Consumer Trends 2025 (The New Consumer / Coefficient Capital)

### TikTok Shop Entertainment-Commerce Flywheel
**Source:** Consumer Trends 2025 (The New Consumer / Coefficient Capital)
**Advisors:** strategist, customer
**One-liner:** TikTok Shop has created a massive marketplace converting passive viewers into impulse buyers at scale.
**Core concept:** 50% of monthly TikTok active users have already made a TikTok Shop purchase. Spending grew 156% year-over-year by Nov 2024. Top categories are fashion (45%), beauty (44%), and electronics (26%), dominated by cheap, unbranded, impulse-driven products.
**Audit application:** An advisor evaluates whether a project has a social commerce strategy mapped to where Gen Z and Millennials actually convert (entertainment-first platforms).
**Red flags it detects:**
- Product targeting Gen Z but has zero social commerce or creator-driven sales strategy
- Team assumes e-commerce only happens on Amazon/Shopify
- Brand positioning is premium but category is being commoditized by ultra-cheap social commerce

### E-commerce Pull-Forward Normalization
**Source:** Consumer Trends 2025 (The New Consumer / Coefficient Capital)
**Advisors:** contrarian, strategist
**One-liner:** E-commerce share of US retail has only just returned to its COVID peak (16.2%), meaning the "digital revolution" in retail is slower than most founders assume.
**Core concept:** US e-commerce hit $1.16 trillion but is only 16.2% of total retail. Pandemic-era digital behavior shifts largely reverted. Physical retail is not dying.
**Audit application:** An advisor pressure-tests any pitch that assumes accelerating online penetration.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Pitch deck cites "e-commerce is taking over retail" without acknowledging 84% still offline
- Business model assumes physical retail is dead
- Growth projections based on pandemic-era adoption rates that have since normalized

### Online Grocery's Affordable Convenience Inflection
**Source:** Consumer Trends 2025 (The New Consumer / Coefficient Capital)
**Advisors:** customer, strategist
**One-liner:** Online grocery is growing at 28% YoY because it has become affordable enough as a convenience layer, not a replacement.
**Core concept:** The unbundling thesis (DTC grocery brands replacing stores) largely failed. Instead, delivery became a supplementary channel on top of in-store habits.
**Audit application:** An advisor checks whether a grocery/CPG startup is positioning as a replacement or a layer.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Grocery startup pitching full replacement of in-store shopping
- Model depends on consumers abandoning physical grocery stores
- No strategy for the "supplementary channel" reality

## What Matters to Today's Consumer 2025 (Capgemini, N=12,000)

### Five Consumer Behavior Clusters Framework
**Source:** What Matters to Today's Consumer 2025 (Capgemini, N=12,000)
**Advisors:** strategist, customer
**One-liner:** K-means clustering reveals five behavioral segments that cut across age demographics, proving Gen Z is not a monolith.
**Core concept:** Educated Rationalists (22%), Convenience-Led Urbanites (30%), Grounded Suburbanites (16%), Digital-First Trailblazers (22%), and Traditionalists (12%). Gen Z appears across three different clusters with different behaviors.
**Audit application:** An advisor challenges any marketing strategy that targets by age alone. Behavior-based segmentation outperforms demographic targeting.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Marketing strategy defines target audience solely by age cohort
- No behavioral segmentation beyond basic demographics
- Assumption that all young consumers are "digital-first"

### Gen AI Search Displacement Signal
**Source:** What Matters to Today's Consumer 2025 (Capgemini, N=12,000)
**Advisors:** strategist, contrarian
**One-liner:** 58% of consumers replaced traditional search engines with Gen AI tools, up from 25% one year ago, but satisfaction is falling.
**Core concept:** Usage is surging while satisfaction is declining -- a dangerous divergence. 68% have purchased products recommended by Gen AI. Growing concern about AI bias (75%), misinformation (73%).
**Audit application:** An advisor evaluates whether a project is building for the Gen AI discovery layer while tracking the satisfaction gap.
**Red flags it detects:**
- SEO-only acquisition strategy with no plan for AI-powered discovery
- Building Gen AI features without measuring satisfaction or trust
- Ignoring growing consumer concern about AI bias and misinformation

### Social Commerce as Primary Gen Z Acquisition Channel
**Source:** What Matters to Today's Consumer 2025 (Capgemini, N=12,000)
**Advisors:** customer, strategist
**One-liner:** 53% of Gen Z purchases new products through social media; social commerce share jumped from 24% to 32% in one year.
**Core concept:** 68% of Gen Z discovered a new product on social media. Quick commerce demand surged: willingness to pay for 2-hour delivery rose from 6% to 9% of order value.
**Audit application:** An advisor checks whether a consumer brand has mapped its acquisition funnel to match the real discovery path: social first, then retail media, then search.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Consumer brand spending most of budget on traditional search/display
- No influencer or creator strategy despite 68% of Gen Z discovering products through social
- Ignoring retail media networks

### Brand Switching and Loyalty Erosion
**Source:** What Matters to Today's Consumer 2025 (Capgemini, N=12,000)
**Advisors:** customer, contrarian
**One-liner:** 70% of consumers switch to try new brands; 65% say loyalty programs have failed to deliver better experiences.
**Core concept:** Consumers are price-rational, information-rich, and willing to switch at any moment. 65% buy private-label or low-cost brands. 73% of millennials demand shrinkflation alerts.
**Audit application:** An advisor stress-tests any business model that depends on customer lock-in or brand loyalty.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Business model assumes high lifetime value without genuine switching cost
- Loyalty program delivers discounts but not differentiated experience
- Premium pricing without transparent value justification

## The Future of Sales with AI (BCG, August 2024)

### AI Sales Maturity Ladder
**Source:** The Future of Sales with AI (BCG, August 2024)
**Advisors:** strategist, contrarian
**One-liner:** B2B sales will evolve through four stages -- age-old, augmented, assisted, autonomous -- with 1.8x margin impact.
**Core concept:** Most companies are between stage 1 and 2. BCG estimates total impact at 1.8x margin: 50% better acquisition, 75% better cross-sell, 40% increase in digital channels, 50% decrease in back-office costs, 25% lower churn.
**Audit application:** An advisor maps any B2B company against this ladder.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Claiming "autonomous AI sales" without the data foundation
- Sales team has no AI tooling while competitors are in augmented/assisted stages
- Technology focus ignoring the 90% people-and-process change

---

### 90/10 Change Management Rule for Sales AI
**Source:** The Future of Sales with AI (BCG, August 2024)
**Advisors:** strategist, contrarian
**One-liner:** Sales AI transformation is 90% change management and 10% technology.
**Core concept:** Typical digital transformations split 70/20/10. Sales AI flips this: 90% depends on change management (leadership, engagement, process, culture, training) and 10% on the tech.
**Audit application:** An advisor evaluates the ratio of change management planning to technology deployment.
**Red flags it detects:**
- AI implementation plan that is entirely about the tech stack
- No leadership activation plan or sales team co-creation
- KPIs not redesigned for AI-augmented productivity

## State of Enterprise Software 2025 (SVB)

### AI Capital Concentration Paradox
**Source:** State of Enterprise Software 2025 (SVB)
**Advisors:** strategist, contrarian
**One-liner:** 45% of US VC goes to AI companies, but exclude AI and there is no meaningful investment uptick.
**Core concept:** AI companies commanded 45% of US VC in enterprise software (up from 9% in 2022). Series A graduation rate continues to decline. Median revenue at Series A bottom quartile in 2024 equals the 2021 median, showing the bar has dramatically risen.
**Audit application:** An advisor evaluates whether a startup has realistic fundraising expectations.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Non-AI startup assuming 2021-era fundraising timelines
- AI startup burning to $100M in ~3 years without sustainable unit economics
- Projecting easy Series A graduation when the rate is at historic lows

---

### Rule of 40 Erosion and SaaS-to-Consumption Shift
**Source:** State of Enterprise Software 2025 (SVB)
**Advisors:** strategist, contrarian
**One-liner:** Median Rule of 40 for $50M+ companies fell from 21% to 9% since 2021; pricing is shifting to hybrid subscription-plus-consumption.
**Core concept:** Only 13% of startups with $50M+ revenue are above Rule of 40 (down from 31% in 2021). Real shift toward layering consumption-based features on top of subscriptions.
**Audit application:** An advisor evaluates growth-profitability trade-off and pricing model.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Projecting 2021-era growth rates
- Purely per-seat pricing when market is moving to consumption-based
- No plan for extending runway when half of startups need to raise within 12 months

---

### Zombiecorn Risk and Exit Bottleneck
**Source:** State of Enterprise Software 2025 (SVB)
**Advisors:** contrarian, strategist
**One-liner:** 300+ unicorns with median age 11.5 years, most unable to IPO -- growing "Zombiecorn" population.
**Core concept:** Revenue multiples at IPO dropped from 15.2x to 4.6x. 71% of US VC-backed startups are too small (<$25M revenue) to IPO. M&A is picking up as the alternative exit.
**Audit application:** An advisor assesses exit strategy realism.
**Red flags it detects:**
- $1B+ valuation but sub-$25M revenue with no profitability path
- Exit strategy depends entirely on IPO
- Over 8 years old, never profitable, no path to Rule of 40

## Understanding Digital Marketing by Damian Ryan

### The Digital Marketing Strategy Framework
**Source:** Understanding Digital Marketing by Damian Ryan
**Advisors:** strategist, customer
**One-liner:** Digital marketing strategy must start with understanding the digital consumer, not the technology.
**Core concept:** Before choosing any channel: (1) define the business's unique position, (2) understand the digital consumer's behavior, (3) adapt the marketing mix for digital, (4) set clear KPIs tied to business objectives. The website is the hub of your digital marketing world.
**Audit application:** An advisor checks whether digital strategy is built from customer understanding outward to channel selection.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Strategy starts with channel selection instead of customer insight
- No defined KPIs connecting digital activity to business outcomes
- Website treated as afterthought rather than central conversion hub

---

### Owned, Paid, and Earned Media Attribution
**Source:** Understanding Digital Marketing by Damian Ryan
**Advisors:** strategist, customer
**One-liner:** Measuring success requires attribution modeling across owned, paid, and earned media, not vanity metrics from one.
**Core concept:** Owned (website, email, app) is controlled. Paid (ads) is rented. Earned (press, reviews, organic) is reputation-driven. Earned is most valuable but hardest to measure.
**Audit application:** An advisor evaluates the media mix and measurement framework.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Marketing budget heavily skewed to paid with no investment in owned assets
- KPIs measuring vanity metrics instead of conversion-linked outcomes
- No attribution model for how channels work together

---

### Content as Natural Selection Engine
**Source:** Understanding Digital Marketing by Damian Ryan
**Advisors:** strategist, customer
**One-liner:** Only the most relevant, valuable content survives algorithm changes and consumer attention filters.
**Core concept:** Content marketing must solve real customer problems, not just generate volume. Content strategy (what and why), content production (quality over quantity), and content promotion (distribution equals creation).
**Audit application:** An advisor checks whether content strategy is built on customer problems and search intent.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Content calendar filled with brand-centric content addressing no customer problem
- High volume with low engagement
- No content distribution strategy

## Best Digital Marketing Campaigns (Ryan & Jones)

### The Five Campaign Selection Criteria
**Source:** Best Digital Marketing Campaigns by Ryan & Jones
**Advisors:** strategist, customer
**One-liner:** Best campaigns are evaluated on: challenge understanding, innovation, creativity, engagement ROI, and "wow" factor.
**Core concept:** ROI is engagement first, financial return second. Key reframe: "a strategy for the digital audience, not a digital strategy for the audience."
**Audit application:** An advisor evaluates campaigns against all five criteria.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Campaign optimized purely for clicks without engagement or emotional resonance
- Technology used for novelty rather than solving engagement problems
- Strategy disconnected from who the audience actually is

## Retail 4.0: Future of Retail Grocery (McKinsey)

### Retail Evolution Eras Framework (1.0 to 4.0)
**Source:** Retail 4.0 by McKinsey
**Advisors:** strategist, contrarian
**One-liner:** Retail transforms once per generation -- from self-service to hypermarkets to e-commerce to omni-channel -- each era creating new winners.
**Core concept:** Retail 1.0 (1916): self-service. 2.0 (1963): hypermarkets. 3.0 (1995): e-commerce. 4.0 (now): omni-channel. Cautionary tale: Webvan tried to scale too fast.
**Audit application:** An advisor maps any retail/commerce startup to the correct era.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Startup claiming "revolutionary" that is actually incremental within Retail 3.0
- Repeating Webvan's mistake: scaling before unit economics
- Ignoring the decades-long pace of retail transformation

---

### The "Endless Aisle" Omni-Channel Model
**Source:** Retail 4.0 by McKinsey
**Advisors:** customer, strategist
**One-liner:** The most successful retailers embed virtual stores within physical ones, but these innovations are often margin-dilutive.
**Core concept:** Examples: Home Plus virtual stores in Korean subways, Walmart To Go, Burberry's "walk-in-website." Key warning: France's drive-through grocery model is less profitable than conventional stores.
**Audit application:** An advisor checks whether omni-channel unit economics are honestly calculated.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Omni-channel strategy that increases convenience but has no margin dilution model
- Physical retailer that hasn't calculated true cost of adding digital channels
- Retail experience focused only on transactions without entertainment layer

---

### Four E-Grocery Customer Archetypes
**Source:** Retail 4.0 by McKinsey
**Advisors:** customer, strategist
**One-liner:** Four segments -- Parents with Young Kids, Tech-Savvy Urbanites, Online Shoppers, Budget-Driven -- each with distinct triggers and delivery preferences.
**Core concept:** Pick 1-2 archetypes and serve them excellently rather than trying to serve all four.
**Audit application:** An advisor maps a grocery startup's strategy against these four archetypes.
**Red flags it detects:**
- Undifferentiated positioning that does not serve any specific archetype
- Marketing treats all online grocery shoppers as the same
- UX does not match the targeted archetype's specific needs
